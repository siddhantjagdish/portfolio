The Ugly App
That Prints Money

A case study in why Amazon refuses to be beautiful, and why the ugliest app on your phone might be the most ruthlessly optimized thing you own.

SJ Siddhant Jagdish 13 min read 2026

Open Blinkit during Diwali and the app greets you with festive skins, smooth cart animations, and a countdown timer that turns a packet of chips into an occasion. Open Amazon to buy a phone charger and the contrast is jarring. Dense text, cluttered listings, a layout that has barely moved in a decade. One looks like a video game. The other looks like a filing cabinet.

The lazy read is that Amazon, a trillion-dollar company, simply cannot find designers who know what a Lottie animation is. The accurate read is far more interesting. Amazon looks the way it does on purpose, and Blinkit, Zepto, and Instamart look the way they do because their business models leave them no other choice. This is a case study in how interface design is downstream of economics, and why the ugliest app on your phone might be the most ruthlessly optimized thing you own.

Part One: The Machine That Ate Design

The foundational fact about Amazon is that design decisions there are not made by designers. They are made by data, at industrial scale.

Amazon runs an internal experimentation platform called Weblab. Jeff Bezos described it years ago as a group constantly experimenting with the user interface, getting statistical data from real usage patterns about which interfaces work best. The trajectory tells the story: 546 experiments in 2011, 1,092 in 2012, 1,976 in 2013. Today the number reportedly exceeds 12,000 experiments a year.

Consider what that implies. Every pixel on that cluttered page has survived a gauntlet. The dense product grid, the wall of reviews, the buy box with seven delivery options. Each element either won an A/B test or was never beaten by anything prettier. One designer analyzing the company put it bluntly: everything you see on Amazon, every pixel, pretty or ugly, is rigorously tested. If the funnel drives profit, aesthetics are a secondary concern.

So the phrase "Amazon's UI is dated" contains a hidden assumption worth examining. It assumes beautiful versions were never tried. They almost certainly were. They lost.

Part Two: The Arithmetic of Standing Still

At hundreds of billions of dollars in GMV, a redesign stops being a creative decision and becomes an actuarial one.

If a fresh, modern interface drops conversion by even one percent, billions evaporate. The upside, meanwhile, is users vaguely feeling that the app looks nicer. Fast Company captured the internal logic in its 2021 investigation: the consequences of significantly redesigning any part of a store that size are almost too great for Amazon's management culture to fathom.

There is also a well-established UX principle quietly working in Amazon's favor. Jakob's Law holds that users spend most of their time on other websites, so they expect yours to work like the ones they already know. The twist is that Amazon is the site everyone else copies. Hundreds of millions of people carry muscle memory for exactly where the buy button sits and exactly how the filters behave. Jakob Nielsen has argued that violating these learned expectations can cost a site roughly 80 percent of its potential users.

For most companies, familiarity is a design constraint. For Amazon, it is a balance-sheet asset.

Part Three: The Inconvenient Truth About the Genius Narrative

Honesty requires complicating the "deliberate genius" story, because it is only half true.

Fast Company interviewed more than a dozen designers who worked at Amazon, several directly with Bezos. One former designer said it plainly: Amazon will never be a design-led company. Customer obsession, it turns out, does not automatically translate into investment in design rigor.

The structural explanation is one every product person will recognize. Amazon runs on small, autonomous two-pizza teams, each shipping independently, each optimizing its own metrics. Conway's Law predicts that products mirror the org charts that build them, and Amazon's homepage is the textbook exhibit: a patchwork of modules, sponsored units, and inconsistent components that no single person art-directs, because no single person can.

Layer on three further constraints. A codebase roughly thirty years old. A catalog of hundreds of millions of SKUs whose images and descriptions are produced by third-party sellers rather than Amazon itself. A global user base spanning cheap phones, slow connections, and every demographic imaginable. A catalog like that cannot be art-directed. It can only be survived, by a utilitarian machine built for exactly that purpose.

Both readings are true simultaneously. Amazon deliberately refuses to trade conversion for beauty, and its structure and legacy would make cohesive beauty nearly impossible even if the appetite existed.

Part Four: The Slot Machine in Your Pocket

Quick commerce is the mirror image of Amazon in almost every variable that matters, and the interface follows directly.

Start with the catalog. Amazon manages hundreds of millions of SKUs it does not control. Blinkit began with roughly 2,000 emergency grocery items and has grown past 30,000. Zepto has built catalogs of around 40,000 SKUs. That is small enough to curate, and because these companies own the inventory in their dark stores, every product image can be made consistent. Thirty thousand items can be art-directed. Three hundred million cannot.

Next, frequency, which is the decisive variable. Per Redseer, quick commerce users place five to six orders a month at average values of 450 to 500 rupees, against three to four monthly orders at 800 to 850 rupees for traditional e-commerce. Quick commerce is not competing for a purchase. It is competing for a habit, twice a day, every day. Habits are built through delight, dopamine, and personality, not through faceted search filters.

Then the basket economics, where the design logic becomes almost mechanical. A rider costs roughly the same 40 to 55 rupees whether the bag contains milk or milk plus chips plus a charger plus ice cream. The entire game is therefore lifting order value through impulse additions, and the apps are engineered accordingly. One-tap add to cart. Dense scrollable grids, with Zepto deliberately packing more SKUs per screen to encourage impulsive buying. Trending near you. Countdown timers. Cashback wheels and flash sales. Datum Intelligence surveyed over 3,000 users and found that 75 percent report increased unplanned purchases since adopting quick commerce. In Mumbai the figure was 82 percent.

The flashiness, in other words, is not decoration. It is the revenue model rendered in pixels.

A final layer completes the picture: advertising. Combined ad revenue for Blinkit, Zepto, and Instamart is projected at roughly 4,900 crore rupees in 2026. Every rich banner, sponsored placement, and festive takeover is monetized surface area. A more engaging app produces more scrolling, and more scrolling produces more ad inventory. Delight, conveniently, is billable.

Part Five: Search Versus Scroll

The cleanest frame for the entire contrast fits in two sentences.

Amazon is search-led. The user arrives with intent, already wanting a charger, and Amazon's job is to avoid losing the sale between intent and checkout. Hence the optimization for findability, trust signals, comparison, and conversion: reviews, ratings, badges, delivery dates, dense information. Utilitarian by design.

Quick commerce is discovery-led. The user frequently opens Blinkit without wanting anything in particular, and the app's job is to manufacture wanting. Hence the optimization for browsing, delight, impulse, and habit. Experiential by design.

The interface follows the shopping mode. Neither is wrong. They are solving different problems, and each would fail spectacularly at the other's.

Part Six: The India Layer

The Indian context adds its own distinct flavor to the divergence.

The core quick commerce demographic is 25 to 35, mobile-first, and highly responsive to brand personality. That personality has a traceable lineage. Blinkit's quirky voice descends from Zomato under Deepinder Goyal, who built one of India's most meme-fluent consumer brands. Goyal has joked that Zepto's quirky marketing was learned from Zomato, a claim made easier by the number of ex-Zomato marketers on Zepto's payroll. Festive theming, meanwhile, is not a gimmick but a strategy: Diwali skins, IPL tie-ins, and Raksha Bandhan pushes have effectively converted the Indian calendar into a permanent shopping occasion.

Amazon's response is its own revealing footnote. Having initially dismissed quick commerce, it launched Amazon Now in Bengaluru in 2025 and is targeting more than 1,000 dark stores by the end of 2026. The telling design decision: Amazon Now lives inside the main Amazon app rather than as a standalone product. Jefferies flagged precisely this, noting that experience suggests higher traction for standalone verticals like Blinkit and that Amazon lacks brand recall in the category. Even while entering a habit-driven, experiential market, Amazon's organizational gravity pulled the product back toward its utilitarian mothership.

Part Seven: Where Both Roads Lead When Nobody Checks the Map

Before the flashy apps are crowned the heroes of design, one uncomfortable symmetry deserves the record.

Amazon paid a 2.5 billion dollar FTC settlement in 2025 over Prime. The cancellation flow was internally code-named the Iliad Flow: four pages, six clicks, and fifteen options standing between a user and escape. According to the FTC, whenever Amazon simplified the flow, Prime signups dropped, so the changes were rolled back. Conversion optimization, followed to its logical conclusion, arrived somewhere ugly.

India's quick commerce players are not clean either. The CCPA fined Zepto 7 lakh rupees for drip pricing and for sneaking items like Zepto Pass into baskets. A 2026 study estimated that dark patterns cost Indian shoppers 25,000 to 28,000 crore rupees annually. The same study contained a delicious irony: Amazon scored best among e-commerce players on dark-pattern severity, and Blinkit scored cleanest in quick commerce. The ugliest app was the most honest one.

Engagement optimization and conversion optimization are, in the end, just optimization. The ethics depend entirely on what is being optimized at the user's expense.

The Case Study Takeaways

Five conclusions survive the evidence.

  • Prettier is not better by default. Amazon has almost certainly tested cleaner layouts and ships the dense ones anyway, because ugly that converts beats beautiful that does not. A redesign that cannot win the primary metric in a controlled test is a vanity project with a Figma file.
  • UI is downstream of the business model. Before judging any app's design, interrogate the fundamentals: purchase frequency, basket economics, catalog size, inventory ownership, intent-led versus discovery-led shopping, and org structure. Answer those and the interface largely designs itself. Blinkit's animations exist because a fixed rider cost demands impulse additions. Amazon's density exists because a thirty-year-old marketplace with three hundred million SKUs demands findability.
  • Copying Amazon is a trap. Its clutter works because Amazon is Amazon: total dominance compounded by decades of user habituation. A challenger copying the layout inherits the clutter without the trust. As one UX teardown observed, Amazon sacrificed basic UX principles to optimize conversion, and that trade is only available from a position of dominance.
  • Legacy and org charts are underrated design forces. Much of what reads as a design decision is actually Conway's Law plus twenty years of dependencies. When an inherited product's interface looks like a mess, the org chart usually deserves the blame before the designers do.
  • Watch the line. The FTC case and the Zepto fine are twin reminders that when a single metric becomes the only thing that matters, design quietly becomes a weapon aimed at the user. Guardrail metrics, complaints, cancellations, and trust exist precisely for this reason.

So the next time Amazon's clutter provokes a wince, the correct reading is not bad design. It is twelve thousand experiments a year, thirty years of legacy, and a company that concluded, with data, that the user's dopamine is not the product. The purchase is. And when Blinkit's Diwali animation charms one more item into the cart, that is not merely good design either. That is a rider's fixed cost being amortized across an impulse.

Both apps are doing exactly what their business models demand. That is the entire case.
